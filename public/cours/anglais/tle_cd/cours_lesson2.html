Describing Friends and Peers

When describing people, we often talk about their physical appearance and moral qualities. We use adjectives to provide details and comparisons to highlight similarities and differences.

Physical Qualities

  • Tall/Short - She is quite tall for her age.
  • Slender/Stocky - He has a stocky build.
  • Curly/Straight hair - Her curly hair is beautiful.
  • Fair/Dark complexion - He has a fair complexion.
  • Sparkling eyes - Her sparkling eyes reveal her joy.

Moral Qualities

  • Honest/Dishonest - An honest person is valued in any community.
  • Kind/Unkind - Her kind nature makes her popular.
  • Generous/Stingy - He's known for being generous with his time.
  • Reliable/Unreliable - A reliable friend is a treasure.
  • Thoughtful/Thoughtless - Her thoughtful gestures are appreciated.

Making Comparisons

We often compare people to highlight similarities and differences. In English, we use specific structures for comparisons:

Type Structure Example
Comparative adjective + -er + than
more + adjective + than		 John is taller than Mark.
Sarah is more generous than her sister.
Superlative the + adjective + -est
the most + adjective		 She is the tallest in the class.
He is the most reliable person I know.
Equality as + adjective + as My brother is as smart as I am.

Mathematical Connection: Comparing Quantities

We can express comparisons mathematically. For example, if Sarah's height is $h_s$ and Mark's height is $h_m$, then:

• Sarah is taller than Mark: $h_s > h_m$

• Mark is shorter than Sarah: $h_m < h_s$

• They are the same height: $h_s = h_m$

Polite Expressions in Social Interactions

Using polite expressions helps build positive relationships and shows respect for others. Here are some common polite expressions:

Greetings
  • "Good morning/afternoon/evening!"
  • "How are you?" / "How do you do?"
  • "It's nice to meet you."
  • "How have you been?"
Everyday Situations
  • "Please" / "Thank you" / "You're welcome"
  • "Excuse me" / "Pardon me"
  • "I'm sorry" / "I apologize"
  • "Would you mind..." / "Could you please..."

Expressing Feelings, Attitudes, and Behaviors

Being able to express emotions and attitudes is crucial for effective communication. Here are some common expressions:

Feelings
  • "I'm thrilled about..."
  • "I'm concerned that..."
  • "I feel disappointed when..."
  • "It frustrates me that..."
Attitudes
  • "In my opinion..."
  • "I believe that..."
  • "From my perspective..."
  • "It seems to me that..."
Behaviors
  • "I tend to..."
  • "I usually..."
  • "I make an effort to..."
  • "I avoid..."

Final Task: Role-play a First Meeting

Now it's time to apply what you've learned! Work with a partner to create and perform a role-play of two classmates meeting for the first time.

Role-play Scenario

Two students meet on the first day of school. They introduce themselves, describe their backgrounds, find common interests, and make plans to study together.

Requirements:
  • Use at least 5 adjectives to describe yourself or each other
  • Include comparisons (e.g., "I'm taller than my brother")
  • Use polite greetings and expressions
  • Express feelings or attitudes about school or hobbies
  • Make plans to meet again
Example Dialogue:

Alex: "Hi, I'm Alex. It's nice to meet you."

Sam: "Nice to meet you too, Alex. I'm Sam. Are you new to this school?"

Alex: "Yes, I just moved here from Springfield. Everyone seems very friendly here."

Sam: "Well, I'm happy to help if you have any questions. I've been here since elementary school."

Alex: "Thanks, I appreciate that. What subjects are you interested in?"
