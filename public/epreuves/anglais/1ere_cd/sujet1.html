Read the passage carefully and answer the questions that follow.

Adapting to New Environments

It's crucial to understand that relocating to a different country can be a challenging experience, even with thorough preparation. You'll encounter numerous aspects that differ from your homeland. As a result, it's highly probable that you'll face adjustment difficulties after several weeks in a new country like Canada. This reaction stems from the shock of adapting to unfamiliar surroundings and intensifies if you experience nostalgia and long for your relatives and companions. It's vital to recognize that this phenomenon is completely normal. It can impact individuals regardless of their origin or destination. Therefore, if you begin to feel despondent or isolated, I urge you to seek assistance. You're welcome to consult with advisors at the International Students Office of the college at any time. They're well-informed about the challenges international students encounter and can offer guidance and support. Now I'll briefly outline some potential difficulties and strategies for managing them.

Firstly, if you're accustomed to a sunny environment, you might find the cold, snowy Canadian winters gloomy, particularly when daylight hours are limited. While we can't alter the climate, we can help you recognize how it might affect your mood.

Another common challenge for international students is cuisine. Local food may differ significantly from what you're familiar with. If you have cooking facilities, I recommend preparing dishes from your home country occasionally. Nevertheless, I caution against excessive consumption of convenience foods, as poor nutrition and sedentary habits will only exacerbate negative feelings. Ensure you incorporate ample fresh produce into your diet and maintain regular physical activity.

Continuous communication in a non-native language can be exhausting, and you might yearn for your mother tongue. You could access streaming services offering content in your language. I suggest reading digital publications or participating in online forums in your native language.

If you experience homesickness, maintain regular communication with home. Schedule frequent video calls or phone conversations. Utilize messaging apps and social media to share updates and images. However, avoid returning home too frequently as this may hinder your adaptation process. Develop friendships with other international students who can relate to your experiences and provide empathy.

Each society has implicit norms governing social interactions. You may find these perplexing and hard to interpret. For instance, Canadians value timeliness, so arriving punctually for appointments is expected. This is why forming connections with domestic students is beneficial. They can assist you in understanding cultural nuances.

Remember, if you feel distressed, support is always available at the office. We also provide a pamphlet for international students summarizing these points, which you may take on your way out.
